Most deaths from exertional heat stroke are preventable. That's not an opinion — it's what the medical literature has been saying for more than twenty years.

Dr Douglas Casa, Director of the Korey Stringer Institute at the University of Connecticut, documented across more than 400 cases that the survival rate for exertional heat stroke reaches 100% when cooling is initiated within the first 30 minutes. Zero deaths. Zero permanent neurological damage.

That result isn't automatic. It depends on two things: recognising the problem in time, and having the means to respond.

---

## Three Windows That Determine the Outcome

Managing exertional heat stroke plays out across three distinct time windows.

### Window 1: Before the Event (Planning)

This is where most critical situations are won or lost. A thermally well-prepared event doesn't eliminate risk — but it dramatically reduces the probability of a case becoming a fatality.

**Essential actions:**

*Monitor thermal indices.* The WBGT (Wet Bulb Globe Temperature) index combines temperature, humidity, and solar radiation. The ACSM recommends progressive action thresholds: programme modifications above 28°C WBGT, cancellation or postponement above 32°C WBGT for endurance events.

*Train the teams.* First responders, volunteers, and race officials must be able to recognise early warning signs: confusion, unsteady gait, abnormal behaviour during exertion. The victim is often the last to realise what is happening.

*Prepare cooling stations.* A forward medical post with cold water immersion equipment must be identified, positioned, and equipped before the first competitor starts. Not during the race.

*Define an emergency action plan.* Who calls emergency services? Who initiates cooling? Who monitors core temperature? These questions should not be asked for the first time when a victim collapses.

### Window 2: During Exertion (Monitoring and Early Detection)

Exertional heat stroke progresses quickly. Early manifestations — unusual fatigue, irritability, headache — can look like normal signs of effort. That's the challenge.

**Signs that cannot wait:**
- Confusion or disorientation, even mild
- Abnormal behaviour (unexplained aggressiveness, incoherent speech)
- Unsteady gait, reduced coordination
- Very flushed complexion or, conversely, sudden pallor with cessation of sweating
- Collapse

As soon as any of these signs appears, exertion must stop immediately. Not gradually. Immediately.

Definitive diagnosis relies on rectal temperature measurement — the only reliable method in prehospital settings according to international consensus. A core temperature above 40°C (104°F) in the presence of neurological signs confirms exertional heat stroke.

### Window 3: The First 30 Minutes (Emergency Treatment)

This is the window that determines prognosis. The ACSM 2023 consensus is explicit: *time is tissue*. Like cardiac arrest or stroke, every minute counts.

The gold-standard treatment is whole-body cold water immersion (between 1.5°C and 15°C / 35°F and 59°F). This method achieves a cooling rate of 0.35°C (0.63°F) per minute — the only rate compatible with the 30-minute therapeutic window.

The *Cool first, transport second* protocol applies without exception: cool on site, then evacuate. Initiating transport before cooling exposes the victim to continued deterioration throughout the journey.

## The Most Common Field Mistakes

Even well-trained teams make mistakes under pressure. Here are the main ones.

**Mistake 1: Transporting before cooling.** The most widespread and most dangerous error. It's often driven by good intentions — getting the victim to hospital quickly — but it systematically worsens outcomes.

**Mistake 2: Relying on insufficient methods.** Wet towels, ice packs on vascular axes, misting: these methods are useful as supplementary measures, but their cooling rate (0.03–0.10°C/min) is insufficient for severe exertional heat stroke. They do not replace immersion.

**Mistake 3: Waiting for diagnostic confirmation.** When in doubt, cool. The risk of unnecessarily cooling someone without exertional heat stroke is infinitely lower than the risk of not cooling someone who has it.

**Mistake 4: Not monitoring core temperature.** Cooling should stop when rectal temperature reaches 38.5–39°C (101.3–102.2°F) to avoid iatrogenic hypothermia. Without a rectal thermometer, that monitoring is impossible.

## Operational Checklist for Event Organisers

To include in any heat emergency plan:

**30 days before**
- [ ] WBGT reference consultation and definition of action thresholds
- [ ] Medical team training on the immersion protocol
- [ ] Identification and equipment of forward medical posts

**7 days before**
- [ ] Weather forecast review and device adjustment if necessary
- [ ] Full team briefing on warning signs
- [ ] Cooling equipment test

**Event day**
- [ ] Cooling equipment in place before the start
- [ ] Ice stock calculated (target: maintain water below 15°C / 59°F)
- [ ] Rectal thermometers available at the medical post
- [ ] Communication protocol with emergency services confirmed
