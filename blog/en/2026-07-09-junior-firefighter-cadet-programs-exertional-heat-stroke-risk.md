Junior firefighter cadet programs exist to build the next generation of firefighters, and they do it by mirroring real training: carrying gear, physical fitness tests, live drills in full turnout gear. That realism is the point, but it also means the exertional heat stroke risk is not limited to full-time operational crews.

## The risk does not wait for a career start

Cadets, often teenagers, take part in load-bearing exercises, timed physical tests, and simulated scenarios that place the same metabolic and thermal load on their bodies as adult crews face. Adolescent physiology adds a layer of risk: teenagers can have less efficient sweat response and slower acclimatization to heat than adults, which is not always factored into cadet program scheduling.

## Why programs underestimate the risk

Cadet programs are frequently run by volunteer instructors focused on discipline, teamwork, and skill-building, not necessarily briefed on the specific signs of exertional heat stroke versus ordinary fatigue. A cadet who becomes confused or disoriented after a hot-weather drill can be mistaken for someone who is simply out of shape or not trying hard enough, a dangerous assumption when the underlying cause is a medical emergency.

## What a cadet program needs on hand

Any program that runs physical drills in turnout gear during warm months should have a rapid cooling capability on site, not just water and shade. The same Cool First, Transport Second protocol that applies to adult crews applies to cadets, and the therapeutic window is identical: cooling within 30 minutes of onset is what the evidence associates with survival.
