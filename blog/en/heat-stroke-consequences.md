Every summer, athletes, soldiers, and workers collapse in the heat. Most of the time it gets called a "heat episode." Sometimes it's far more serious.

Exertional heat stroke is a life-threatening emergency. It doesn't build slowly — it can go from a stumble to a coma in under an hour. And yet it remains under-recognised, under-treated, and critically under-equipped.

---

## What Happens Inside the Body

During intense physical exertion, the body generates enormous amounts of heat. Under normal conditions, sweating and peripheral blood circulation dissipate that heat and keep core temperature stable.

When those thermoregulatory mechanisms are overwhelmed — by high ambient temperature, humidity, exertion intensity, or their combination — core temperature starts rising.

At 38.5°C (101.3°F), the first warning signs appear. At 40°C (104°F), the diagnostic threshold for exertional heat stroke is crossed. At 42°C (107.6°F), cellular damage becomes irreversible.

According to a review published in *Critical Care* (Walter & Carraretto, 2016), the brain is the organ most vulnerable to hyperthermia. A single hyperthermic episode can cause short-term neurological and cognitive dysfunction — which may become prolonged or permanent. The cerebellum is particularly intolerant to heat.

---

## The Consequences: Far Beyond a "Heat Episode"

The immediate effects of severe exertional heat stroke are well-documented:

**Neurological** — confusion, agitation, seizures, coma. Cognitive dysfunction can persist for weeks after the acute episode. In severe cases, permanent cerebellar damage has been reported.

**Cardiovascular** — tachycardia, haemodynamic instability, hypotension. According to the ACSM Expert Consensus Statement (2023), exertional heat illness survivors face a 3.9 times higher incidence of major cardiovascular events, 5.5 times greater risk of ischaemic stroke, and a 15 times higher incidence of atrial fibrillation over a 14-year follow-up period.

**Renal** — rhabdomyolysis (breakdown of muscle fibres) releases myoglobin that obstructs renal tubules. Acute kidney injury can develop within hours.

**Hepatic and coagulatory** — disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), acute liver failure, multi-organ failure.

A cohort mortality study of US Army personnel hospitalised for exertional heat illness, cited by the ACSM (2023), found a 40% increased long-term mortality risk compared to a reference population.

Without adequate treatment within the first 30 minutes, both vital and neurological prognosis are directly at stake.

## Who Is at Risk?

Exertional heat stroke doesn't only affect elite athletes. The at-risk population is broader than commonly assumed:

- **Endurance athletes** (marathon, trail running, triathlon) in hot and humid conditions
- **Military personnel** during intensive training, selection exercises, or operations in hot climates
- **Firefighters** during prolonged interventions, particularly wildfire operations
- **Outdoor workers** exposed to heat (construction, metallurgy, agriculture, logistics)
- **Team sport players** (football, rugby) during summer pre-season training

Aggravating factors include dehydration, insufficient heat acclimatisation, certain medications (diuretics, beta-blockers, antipsychotics), pre-existing cardiovascular conditions, and obesity.

## Prevention: What Actually Works

Prevention remains the first line of defence. Recommendations from the ACSM, the IOC (Hosokawa, Racinais et al., BJSM 2021), and leading prehospital medicine bodies converge on several key measures:

**Before exertion**
- Progressive heat acclimatisation (10–14 day gradual protocol)
- Adequate hydration in the hours before exercise
- Avoiding intense exertion during peak heat hours (11am–4pm)
- Monitoring WBGT (Wet Bulb Globe Temperature) indices at event venues

**During exertion**
- Regular fluid intake — don't wait for thirst
- Peer monitoring — early signs (confusion, unsteady gait) are often spotted by others before the victim themselves
- Appropriate clothing, intensity reduction at the first warning signs
- Intermediate cooling stations on long-distance routes

**Organisation and logistics**
- Training medical staff and first responders to recognise and treat exertional heat stroke
- Pre-defined emergency action plans for any thermally at-risk event
- Cooling equipment available on site — before the event starts

---

## When Prevention Isn't Enough

Even with solid preventive measures in place, cases happen. Heat, exertion, individual physiology — the risk factors are never fully controllable.

This is where emergency treatment becomes decisive.

Initial management rests on stopping activity and initiating rapid cooling, ideally through cold water immersion. The *Cool first, transport second* protocol recommends cooling the victim on site before any transfer to a hospital. Every minute of transport without active cooling worsens the prognosis.

Cold water immersion is the only method capable of achieving the cooling rate needed to respect the 30-minute therapeutic window. Alternative methods — wet towels, localised ice packs, misting — are insufficient for severe exertional heat stroke.
